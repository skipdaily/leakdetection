DIY Leak Detection

How to Check Your Water Meter for Leaks

Your water meter can be a valuable tool in detecting hidden leaks. Here's a simple procedure you can follow:

  1. Turn off all water-using appliances in your home (including ice makers, water softeners, etc.).
  2. Locate your water meter and record the current reading or take a photo of it.
  3. Wait 2 hours without using any water in your home.
  4. Check the meter again. If the reading has changed, you likely have a leak somewhere in your system.

While this method can indicate if you have a leak, professional detection services are needed to locate the exact source.

How to Test Your Toilet for Leaks

Toilets are a common source of water leaks. Here's how you can test yours:

  1. Remove the tank lid and add several drops of food coloring to the water in the tank.
  2. Don't flush the toilet and wait 15-20 minutes.
  3. Check the toilet bowl. If you see colored water in the bowl, your toilet has a leak.
  4. The most common cause is a worn flapper that needs replacement - an easy and inexpensive fix.

A leaking toilet can waste hundreds of gallons of water per day, significantly increasing your water bill.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I have my home checked for leaks?

We recommend annual leak inspections for most homes, with more frequent checks if your home is older (15+ years), has a history of plumbing issues, or if you notice warning signs like unexplained increases in utility bills.

How can I tell if I have a water leak?

Common signs include unexplained increases in water bills, the sound of running water when no fixtures are in use, damp or discolored walls or ceilings, musty odors, or visible mold growth. Your water meter can also help detect leaks (see our DIY guide above).

What should I do if I smell gas?

If you smell gas, take immediate action: 1) Don't turn on/off any electrical switches or create any sparks, 2) Open windows and doors to ventilate, 3) Leave the property immediately, 4) Call your gas company's emergency line or 911 from outside or a neighbor's home.

Are carbon monoxide detectors necessary?

Absolutely. Carbon monoxide is colorless and odorless, making it impossible to detect without proper equipment. We strongly recommend installing CO detectors on every level of your home and near sleeping areas. Test them monthly and replace batteries annually.

Will my homeowner's insurance cover water damage from leaks?

Most homeowner's insurance policies cover sudden and accidental water damage but may not cover damage from gradual leaks or neglected maintenance issues. Review your policy and consider additional coverage if needed. Regular professional inspections can help document that you're properly maintaining your home.
